\section{Related Works}

A review of literature was conducted to explore the available research in the area of 
classification of bird species, fine-grained visual recognition, and citizen science 
platforms. The selected literature was critically reviewed and analyzed based on their 
choice of architecture of machine learning algorithm, approaches for dataset creation 
and evaluation, and performance results.

Traditional bird classification systems were typically based on classic machine learning
techniques, such as SVM and Random Forest classifiers. These systems would often depend
on manually designed features, such as color histograms, shape, and texture descriptors.
However, these techniques would have limited success in dealing with practical
image databases characterized by varying illumination, background, image quality,
and bird poses \cite{kambali2024automated, lolge2025evolution}. Recent studies
demonstrate that CNN architectures can significantly improve the results compared to
traditional machine learning due to their ability to automatically extract hierarchical
visual features from the image data \cite{kambali2024automated}.

Bird species classification is commonly considered a Fine-Grained Visual Classification
(FGVC) problem because different bird species may share highly similar visual
characteristics. In FGVC tasks, models must distinguish subtle local differences such as
feather patterns, beak structure, wing shape, and color variations. Chen et al. emphasize
that successful FGVC systems require models capable of learning discriminative local
features and fine visual details \cite{chen2024fetfgvc}. Modern deep learning architectures,
particularly CNN- and Transformer-based models, provide the performance necessary for
handling these complex classification tasks.

There have been several studies regarding the comparative analysis of the performance of different
CNN models in classifying birds species. For instance, the study by Bilgin et al. conducted a 
comparative analysis of three different architectures such as VGG16, ResNet50, and DenseNet.
It was noted that DenseNet and ResNet performed better compared to VGG16 because they had better
feature propagation and gradient flow \cite{bilgin2022bird}. Another example is the study conducted
by Tang, which compares VGG, ResNet, InceptionV3, and MobileNet and shows that ResNet and Inception 
achieve better F1 scores, and MobileNet gives better computational efficiency \cite{tang2025comparative}.

Apart from the accuracy of classification, recent developments have also focused on
inference speed and model size \cite{lolge2025evolution, tang2025comparative}.
Models such as MobileNet proved useful where the deployment of the model is done
on websites and mobile applications. However, dense and residual neural network models
tend to perform more effectively but at the cost of increased memory and computational
requirements \cite{bilgin2022bird}. In our case, we were constrained by this factor,
since the model had to be deployed as part of a live website.

The literature survey also included public datasets containing images of birds, as well as citizen
science platforms. Biodiversity datasets available at a large-scale include the Global Biodiversity 
Information Facility (GBIF) \cite{gbif2025}. The datasets made by these resources are freely 
available and consist of records collected from all around the world.

Citizen science has transformed biodiversity monitoring by enabling decentralized data
collection from large communities of volunteers. Fraisl et al. discuss how citizen science
has become an important component of environmental and ecological research due to its
ability to collect large-scale observational data \cite{fraisl2022citizen}. Similarly,
Bonney et al. explain that citizen science projects can improve public engagement and
scientific awareness while contributing valuable environmental data
\cite{bonney2015citizen}. However, citizen science data may also introduce challenges,
including observer bias, class imbalance, inconsistent image quality, and incorrect
species labels \cite{johnston2022outstanding}. Baker et al. mention that automated
verification and validation techniques are increasingly being used to improve the quality
and reliability of citizen science datasets \cite{baker2021verification}.

The results obtained from the literature review influenced many design choices made in the 
project. These results helped determine which architectures were suitable for selection, which 
criteria should be used for performance evaluation, and which data preprocessing steps should 
be taken. Particular attention was paid to issues such as class imbalance, fine-grained species 
similarity, and varying image quality.
