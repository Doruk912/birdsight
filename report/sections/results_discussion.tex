\section{Results and Discussion}

This section describes the outcomes of the machine learning experiments, 
the final model performance on the dataset and proof that the BirdSight 
web platform functions as expected in the citizen science workflow. The 
results are then discussed in terms of the stated research questions.

\subsection{Experiment and Validation}

Before presenting the results on the interface and numerical aspects, the chapter 
describes what was validated and how it was implemented. Validation was twofold: 
a controlled machine learning protocol with fixed data pods and shared evaluation 
code; and a practical software check showing that the integrated services work 
together as a system.

\subsubsection{Machine learning protocol}

The bird species classifier was developed and assessed over a standardised experimental 
protocol to ensure fair comparisons. Images were extracted from GBIF-mediated observation 
records with species-level tags, restricted to a species list focused on Turkey 
\cite{gbif2025,ebird2025}. The dataset was split into training, validation and testing 
sets using a fixed ratio after preprocessing and cleaning.

We trained five convolutional architectures (VGG16, ResNet50, DenseNet121, MobileNetV2, 
InceptionV3) using the same top-level training method, with ImageNet initialisation method. 
This is the first controlled experiment on the selection of architecture. The InceptionV3 
was chosen and improved the original 23-class setup with three targeted erosion-style 
experiments: label correction, stronger data enhancement and adjustment of the number of 
frozen backbone periods. Subsequently, Finally, the best combined configuration was re-trained 
on an extended 30-class dataset.

In order to reduce overfitting during the fine-tuning stage with early stop, validation 
depended on conserved validation accuracy and loss throughout training. The same measure 
definitions were used to produce the final reported metrics in the untouched testing part.

\subsubsection{Software validation}

The integrated system was validated as a runnable end-to-end application, allowing users 
to authenticate, submit observations with images and coordinates, request species 
recommendations, browse observations and taxonomy pages, and contribute descriptions that update 
observation quality metadata. These checks demonstrate that the architectural separation works 
together in a local deployment configuration.

\subsection{Results}

The project's quantifiable outcomes are summed up in this section. The comparative benchmark 
results across five CNN architectures are shown first, followed by the specific enhancements 
made to InceptionV3, the final performance following the extension to 30 species, and qualitative 
data gathered from the implemented web interface.

\subsubsection{Benchmark comparison (five architectures)}

Out of the five contenders, InceptionV3 had the best overall top 1 accuracy as well as the 
best top 3 and top 5 behavior, as shown in Table~\ref{tab:model_evaluation_results}, which highlights the benchmark performance 
in the separated test data. This is crucial since it allows users to select from the recommended 
species rather than simply accepting the first classification. Due to its lightweight architecture, 
MobileNetV2 remained competitive, but DenseNet121 and ResNet50 fared similarly and marginally 
worse than InceptionV3. VGG16 trailed behind. This aligns with the research that compares 
residual and factored designs with legacy deep stacks \cite{bilgin2022bird,tang2025comparative}.
A compressed visual comparison between the models is given in Figure~\ref{fig:model_comparison_chart}. 
Figure~\ref{fig:inception_confusion_matrix} shows that most of the mass lies on the diagonal for 
InceptionV3 and Figure~\ref{fig:top_confused_pairs} shows a small cluster of frequently confused 
species pairs. Most of these pairs are visually qualitatively similar taxa as expected from 
detailed classification \cite{chen2024fetfgvc}.


\subsubsection{InceptionV3 improvement experiments}

The three follow-up experiments on InceptionV3 show that the largest improvement came from 
stronger data augmentation. The training and validation accuracy curves became more consistent 
compared to the baseline boosting setup (Figure~\ref{fig:baseline_training_history} 
and~\ref{fig:enhanced_aug_training_history}). The effect of freeze-period adjustment was moderate.  
Eleven frozen periods achieved the best validation accuracy of the scan 
(Table~\ref{tab:freeze_epoch_experiment} and Figure~\ref{fig:freeze_epoch_comparison}). Label 
smoothing supported a better calibrated probability but smaller movement, best at 1 accuracy. 
This may be critical in providing successive advice to non-experts.

\subsubsection{Final model on the 30-class dataset}

The final InceptionV3 model was trained on 30 types and 150,000 photos after the optimal settings 
were combined. The test results are shown in Table~\ref{tab:final_optimized_results}. The model maintained a high first-3 
accuracy rate and a solid first-1 accuracy despite the increase of classes. This demonstrates 
that while full first-1 classification is challenging, the sequential suggestion interface is 
still helpful.

\subsection{Discussion}

This section situates BirdSight's findings within the context of the specific project: how the 
InceptionV3 metrics and the implemented platform relate to the research questions, how key design 
choices relate to the cited literature, and which limitations remain honest.

\subsubsection{Answering the research questions}

\textbf{RQ1} asked how adding a bird species classifier to a citizen-science platform can help large-scale 
monitoring. The project results suggest three main points. First, the model gives ranked species suggestions, 
which makes it easier for users to label difficult photos. Second, the observation list, map, and 
identification pages turn uploads into records with location and date, so data can be collected at scale 
\cite{fraisl2022citizen}. Third, the platform does not treat every model guess as final: observations stay 
at \textit{Needs ID} until enough users agree on the same species-level identification. When that agreement 
is reached, the record becomes \textit{Research Grade}. 

\textbf{RQ2} determining whether publicly available bird images are suitable for training deep 
learning models, a large dataset was generated using the GBIF tool \cite{gbif2025}. The final model was then 
pre-processed, split, and carefully trained. A strong performance was achieved after training, supporting 
its suitability. Citizen science images vary in terms of exposure, distance, blur, and 
background interference. Therefore, interference remains in some species even with strong metrics 
(Figure~\ref{fig:top_confused_pairs}).

\subsubsection{Relation to prior work and design choices}

In comparison rankings, comparative CNN studies that select modern architectures for challenging visual 
classification instead of VGG-style cluttered architecture are consistent.\cite{bilgin2022bird,
tang2025comparative,lolge2025evolution}. Decoupling the inference to a separate FastAPI service complies 
with deployment guidelines \cite{karanam2026microservice}. Model lifecycles are kept independent of 
underlying application services.

\subsubsection{Limitations and threats to validity}

The scope is intentionally bounded: 30 species common in Turkey, not a global avifauna model. 
Performance metrics are computed on a specific train/val/test split; although the split is stratified 
and seeded for reproducibility, geographic or temporal leakage in aggregated public datasets can 
never be ruled out completely without additional auditing. External reverse geocoding depends on 
third-party availability and fair-use constraints \cite{nominatim2025}. Finally, the reported UI 
validation is functional rather than a controlled usability experiment with statistical participant 
outcomes.

\subsubsection{Concluding remarks}

Overall, the quantitative results demonstrate that a comparatively strong bird classifier can be 
trained from large-scale public imagery and integrated into a modular web stack. The qualitative 
screenshots demonstrate that the platform channels model output into a workflow where volunteers 
can still discuss, correct, and confirm identifications, which is central to trustworthy citizen 
science at scale \cite{baker2021verification}.
